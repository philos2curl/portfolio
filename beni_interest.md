## What interests you about working for this company?

Most AI health companies are still in pitch mode. Beni is live, with paying customers and real clinical interactions already happening. That's the starting line I want to work from. There's something fundamentally different about joining a product that has already proven it can operate in a clinical environment versus one that's still making the case it could. The hard part — earning trust inside a primary care practice — is already done. The next part, rebuilding the experience layer into something that feels less like software and more like a care team member, is exactly the kind of 0→1 problem I'm built for.

What specifically pulls me in is the translation layer between clinical reality and AI capability. That's not a technical problem — it's a product problem. Knowing where the AI can reliably act and where a care team needs to stay in the loop, designing conversation flows that a patient trusts in a vulnerable moment, making clinical signal actionable without adding cognitive load — those require someone who can hold both sides at once. That's the work I want to be doing.

## What's your hands-on experience with AI agents and tools? How does the way you use or build them set you apart?

I work with AI tools the way the job description is asking for — not as a feature owner who delegates to engineers, but as someone who builds directly. I've integrated LLMs into workflows using Ollama and built RAG pipelines from scratch to understand retrieval behavior under real conditions, not just in demos. I use Claude Code as part of my daily workflow — right now I'm using it to build and deploy my portfolio, including agentic interactions and front-end prototyping I'm writing myself.

What I think sets me apart is that I've developed genuine opinions about where agentic approaches earn their place versus where they introduce risk. In a healthcare context that matters more than anywhere else. I've thought hard about designing around LLM failure modes — hallucination, overconfidence, ambiguous outputs — especially in high-stakes interactions where a wrong answer isn't just a bad user experience. I approach model selection and tool use patterns as product decisions, not engineering ones. And I stay ahead of the space not by reading about it, but by building things in it.

## What is your experience in the healthcare space?

I spent four years at HCA HealthTrust as a Product Manager for a Group Purchasing Operations suite that processed $4.4 billion in annual transactions. My work there sat at the intersection of clinical operations, procurement, and compliance — managing a product catalog and contract lifecycle that touched supply chain decisions across a large hospital network. I learned how healthcare organizations make decisions: slowly, with multiple stakeholders, with a hard floor on risk tolerance. That instinct — knowing when to push and when to hold — is something you can't get outside a regulated environment.

That experience also gave me fluency in the bureaucratic and compliance layer that makes healthcare product work harder than most. EMR integration requirements, documentation standards, the weight of decisions that affect patient safety — I understand why those guardrails exist, and I know how to build inside them without treating compliance as a blocker rather than a design constraint.
