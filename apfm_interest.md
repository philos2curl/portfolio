# Why A Place for Mom Interests Me

I spent four years at HCA HealthTrust managing a product catalog that processed $4.4 billion in annual transactions. Healthcare decisions at that scale taught me something: the higher the stakes, the more the product has to earn trust at every step. A Place for Mom is that same problem on the consumer side — families in one of the most emotionally loaded moments of their lives, trying to make a decision they've never made before and can't afford to get wrong. That's exactly the kind of product challenge I want to be solving.

The agentic angle is what makes this role distinct. I've been building with AI tools hands-on — RAG pipelines, LLM integrations, Ollama — not just managing teams that do it, but doing it myself. The job description asks if you've built something with Claude Code on a weekend because you couldn't help yourself. The answer is yes: I'm using it right now to build a portfolio that demonstrates exactly the kind of PM thinking this role requires. I form opinions about when agentic approaches are the right call versus when they add noise, and I've learned to design around the messy reality of LLM outputs — especially in high-stakes contexts where hallucination isn't just annoying, it's harmful.

What draws me to the timing is that APFM is evolving from a referral network into an intelligent care platform. That transition — from directory to advisor — is the same move I've been making at B&H: from a search and navigation tool into something that actually helps users make decisions, not just find options. I've shipped conversational features, personalization layers, and matching logic on a platform doing hundreds of millions in transactions. The scale and the emotional weight here are different, but the product instincts transfer directly.

I also genuinely believe in the mission. Helping a family find the right place for a parent isn't a transactional decision — it's one of the most personal things a person can do. Building products that make that process clearer and less frightening is the kind of work that feels worth doing.
