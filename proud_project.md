# A Project I'm Proud Of: Condition Rating System Overhaul — B&H Photo

**The motivation**

B&H Photo has a robust used goods business — customers buy and sell pre-owned camera equipment, lenses, and electronics through the platform. For years, the condition rating system (like most in the industry) relied on broad, text-based labels: Excellent, Very Good, Good, Fair. The problem was that those labels meant different things to different people, and what the customer expected when they clicked "Excellent" wasn't always what arrived in the box.

The signal was clear in our customer satisfaction data: the used department had a persistent gap between listed condition and received product that was dragging down CSAT scores and generating returns. But it was the kind of problem that's easy to rationalize — "customers should read the descriptions" — rather than solve. No one had audited the full funnel with fresh eyes.

I decided to own it.

**What I did**

I started by mapping the entire Sell Used funnel from the moment a seller submits an item through grading, listing, and final customer receipt. I was looking for every place where information was lost, compressed, or mistranslated between what the seller described and what the buyer understood.

What I found was that the condition labels were doing too much work with too little structure. There was no visual reference, no standardized grading criteria enforced at intake, and no feedback loop between customer complaints and the grading team. A seller's "Excellent" and a buyer's "Excellent" weren't the same thing — and our system had no mechanism to close that gap.

I built a prototype of an image upload flow designed to make condition visible rather than described. Instead of relying on a seller to choose a label accurately, the prototype captured standardized photos at key inspection points — creating a visual record that traveled with the item all the way to the listing. This gave buyers evidence, not just claims.

I also designed a revised condition taxonomy that tied each tier to explicit, visual criteria rather than vague descriptors — with examples customers could actually use to calibrate expectations before purchase.

**The challenge**

The hardest part wasn't the product design — it was making the case internally. Used goods operations at B&H involve a physical intake process, a grading team with established habits, and a supply chain that moves fast. Adding image capture and stricter criteria to that flow meant asking operations to change something that technically worked. The resistance was real.

I addressed it by building the business case in both directions: showing the cost of the current problem (CSAT drag, return rate, customer trust erosion in a category that depends on repeat buyers) alongside a conservative model of what improvement looked like. Then I packaged it into a deck for the COO — not a product pitch, but a business conversation.

**How I measured success**

The project was in flight when I moved into the B2B & Used role, so the full post-launch measurement is still ahead. But the projected outcome from our baseline analysis pointed to an 8.5% CSAT improvement in the Used category — driven by reducing the expectation gap at the point of listing. We also used return rate and "item not as described" complaint volume as leading indicators to track early signal before CSAT moved.

What I'm most proud of isn't the metric — it's that I took a problem that was easy to ignore and made it impossible to overlook. The COO presentation wasn't the goal; it was the result of doing the work rigorously enough that the business case spoke for itself.
